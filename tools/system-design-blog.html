Crack the System Design Interview

A Step-by-Step Framework — Most engineers fail not because they don't know the concepts. They fail because they don't have a framework.

The complete playbook for software engineers preparing for FAANG and product company system design rounds.

Here is what a typical system design interview looks like.

The interviewer says: "Design Twitter."

You panic. You start drawing boxes. You mention load balancers because they sound right. You talk about databases without explaining why. Forty-five minutes later, the interviewer thanks you and you walk out unsure what just happened.

You knew the concepts. You have read about Kafka, Redis, and sharding. But you had no structure. No framework. No way to turn scattered knowledge into a coherent design.

That is what this guide fixes.

The 8-Step System Design Framework

Use this exact order in every interview. Every single time.

The 8-Step Framework — follow this order in every system design interview
Step 1 · 5 min

Clarify Requirements

Never start designing before you understand what you are building. Ask these questions:

  • Functional: What are the core features? What are we NOT building?
  • Non-functional: How many users? Read-heavy or write-heavy? Latency targets?
  • Scale: Daily active users, global vs regional, consistency requirements?
The interview move: Write the requirements on the whiteboard. Reference them throughout. This signals you are disciplined — not reactive.
Step 2 · 3 min

Estimate Scale

Back-of-the-envelope calculations tell you what kind of system you need before you design it.

Scale estimation for Twitter — these 3 numbers shape every architecture decision
The interview move: Say the numbers out loud as you calculate. Show your reasoning. A wrong estimate explained clearly beats a right answer pulled from thin air.
Step 3 · 3 min

Define the API

Before drawing any architecture, define the contract. What does the system expose to the outside world?

POST   /tweets                              → Create a tweet
GET    /tweets/{id}                         → Get a tweet
GET    /users/{id}/feed                     → Get home feed
POST   /users/{followerId}/follow/{id}      → Follow a user

This forces clarity. If you cannot define the API, you do not yet understand the problem.

Step 4 · 5 min

Design the Data Model

Database selection guide — always explain WHY, not just which one
Step 5 · 10 min

High-Level Architecture

Now draw the system. Start simple. Add complexity only when you have justified it.

Twitter high-level architecture — start simple, add components only when justified
Step 6 · 10 min

Deep Dive: The Fan-out Problem

When a user with 10 million followers posts a tweet, how do you update 10 million feeds?

There is no perfect answer — there is a reasoned choice with understood consequences
The interview move: Present all three options. Then recommend the hybrid. Explain the trade-off. This is what separates senior candidates from everyone else.
Step 7 · 5 min

Address Non-Functional Requirements

Go back to your requirements from Step 1. Prove your design satisfies them.

  • Scalability: Stateless web servers → add instances behind the load balancer. Cassandra → add nodes as data grows.
  • Availability: Load balancer health checks → routes around failed servers. DB replication → promote replica if master fails.
  • Latency: Redis cache → feed reads in <1ms. CDN → media served from edge, 50ms instead of 500ms.
  • Consistency: Eventual consistency is acceptable for feeds. Strong consistency required for payments and auth.
Step 8 · 3 min

Identify Bottlenecks and Trade-offs

End by showing you understand the limits of your own design. Every senior engineer knows their system's weakest points.

"The write amplification in fan-out on write will be a bottleneck at extreme follower counts. We mitigate it with the hybrid approach but it adds operational complexity."

The Most Common Mistakes

🚀
Jumping to solutions
The interviewer says "design Instagram" and you immediately say "we need Kafka." You do not know that yet. Spend the first 8 minutes on requirements and scale estimation.
🔇
Ignoring the interviewer
System design is a conversation. They are giving you signals. When they ask a question, it is usually a hint that you missed something.
🏷️
Only naming technologies
"We'll use Redis." Why Redis? What are you caching? What is your eviction policy? The name is worth nothing without the reasoning.
🕳️
Going too deep too early
Engineers get excited about one component and spend 30 minutes there. Then they have no time for the rest of the system. Follow the time allocations above.
✏️
Not drawing
Draw the architecture. Label the arrows. Name the data flows. A picture communicates in 10 seconds what takes 3 minutes to explain verbally.
